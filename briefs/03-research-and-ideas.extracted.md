Research And Idea Bank
This document synthesizes the main research themes behind the product strategy and turns them into concrete design ideas.
Core Research Themes
1. Cognitive Load Must Be Reduced
ADHD and neurodivergent UX guidance consistently emphasizes that users struggle not only with distraction, but with sequencing, parsing, and staying grounded inside overly dense or surprising interfaces.[cite:110][cite:115][cite:116] Interfaces that reduce visual competition, simplify navigation, and use progressive disclosure support better completion and lower abandonment.[cite:111][cite:112][cite:175]
2. Predictability Matters
Predictable flows help users maintain a mental model of what happens next. Progress indicators, stable labels, and clear states are especially important for people who may lose momentum when interfaces behave inconsistently.[cite:110][cite:175]
3. Emotional Safety Is A Design Requirement
Psychologically safe UX is associated with calm, trust, and confidence rather than pressure, confusion, or shame. Emotionally safe design becomes especially important when users already feel overwhelmed or behind.[cite:174][cite:175]
4. Onboarding Should Reach First Value Fast
Best-practice onboarding patterns emphasize getting the user to their first meaningful success quickly rather than gathering lots of information upfront. Minimal steps, reduced friction, and clearer activation paths improve adoption and retention.[cite:157][cite:158]
5. Latency Must Be Honest
Voice and conversational UX research shows that unexplained delay harms trust quickly. Users tolerate waiting better when the product explains what is happening, shows progress, and offers alternatives when waits become too long.[cite:159][cite:160]
High-Potential Product Ideas
Idea 1: Calm Upload Start
The first actionable screen should focus on one thing only: getting a reading into the system. Remove all optional settings until later, and make the upload box feel supportive rather than technical.[cite:157][cite:175]
Idea 2: “How It Works” In 3 Steps
A short visual strip can immediately reduce uncertainty:
Upload your reading
Pick your voice
Press play and focus
This format aligns with research favoring structured, digestible flows over dense explanatory blocks.[cite:116][cite:157]
Idea 3: Clear Voice Trade-Offs
Let the user choose between Standard and HD with visible trade-offs. This supports autonomy and reduces the feeling that the product is arbitrarily slow.[cite:159][cite:160]
Idea 4: Reassuring Microcopy
Use short lines that reduce emotional friction, such as:
Your reading stays private.
You can change this later.
HD takes longer by design.
You are one step from listening.
These patterns support trust, clarity, and self-efficacy.[cite:174][cite:175]
Idea 5: Optional Sound, Not Ambient Noise
Sound can reinforce success if it is short, soft, and optional, but autoplay or repetitive sounds are likely to increase load rather than help focus.[cite:147][cite:148][cite:151]
Idea 6: Calm Digital Brand System
The visual identity should feel modern and digital without becoming psychedelic or overstimulating. Restrained accents, gentle glow, and stable structure are more aligned with neurodivergent-friendly design than loud rainbow motion.[cite:165][cite:170]
Risks To Avoid
Too many choices on first visit.[cite:116][cite:110]
Vague hero copy that sounds like generic AI marketing.[cite:157]
HD voice that appears broken because waiting is unexplained.[cite:159][cite:160]
Page-load sounds, hover sounds, or aggressive click sounds.[cite:148][cite:151]
Heavy animated visuals that compete with focus.[cite:165][cite:169]
Long forms before the first useful result.[cite:157][cite:158]
