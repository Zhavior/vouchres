Master Product Plan
This plan outlines the first build strategy for a calm, ADHD-friendly reading-to-audio web product. The core idea is to help users turn dense reading into playable audio quickly, with low friction, high trust, and a predictable sense of progress.
Product Goal
The product should help an overwhelmed student or reader move from “I cannot get through this” to “I am listening and making progress” in the fewest possible steps. Research on ADHD-friendly design consistently emphasizes reduced cognitive load, predictable flows, and progressive disclosure rather than cluttered choice-heavy experiences.[cite:116][cite:110][cite:115]
Positioning
The clearest positioning is a calm study companion rather than a flashy AI tool. Neurodivergent-friendly guidance emphasizes emotional safety, low surprise, and interfaces that reduce overstimulation instead of adding theatrical motion or dense decision-making.[cite:111][cite:174][cite:175]
Core Promise
Turn dense readings into audio you can actually finish.

Phase 1: Landing Page
The landing page should communicate one promise, one primary action, and one fast explanation of how the product works. ADHD-focused UX guidance recommends minimizing clutter, reducing competing messages, and making next steps obvious within seconds.[cite:116][cite:110][cite:112]
Landing Page Structure
Hero with one headline, one subheadline, one CTA.
A 3-step “how it works” strip.
A trust section covering privacy, ease, and expected output.
A product preview or listening demo.
A simple closing CTA.
Hero Direction
Headline: Turn dense readings into audio you can actually finish.
Subheadline: Built for students, ADHD brains, and anyone who focuses better by listening.
Primary CTA: Upload a reading
Secondary CTA: See how it works
Phase 2: Signup And Onboarding
The onboarding goal is first value, not profile completion. Strong onboarding patterns guide users quickly toward activation and avoid asking for unnecessary data before the user experiences the product’s benefit.[cite:157][cite:158]
Onboarding Flow
Welcome screen with Google and email options.
Minimal signup form.
Success state with one clear next action.
First upload flow.
Optional preferences after first success.
Rules
No long questionnaires before first use.
One task per screen.
Clear progress markers.
Friendly error handling.
Large mobile-first tap targets.
Phase 3: Voice And HD Experience
Voice generation latency must feel honest and well-managed. Voice UX research shows that users become impatient when delays are unexplained, but tolerate them better when the system signals progress, offers fallback choices, and sets realistic expectations.[cite:159][cite:160]
Voice Strategy
Standard voice: faster, clearly labeled.
HD voice: slower, premium, clearly labeled.
Explicit explanation of trade-offs.
Progress states that feel informative, not frozen.
“Switch to Standard” fallback if HD takes too long.
Progress Language
Preparing your audio
Generating voice
Finalizing playback
Ready to listen
Phase 4: Sound Policy
Sound should support clarity, not stimulation. Accessibility guidance discourages autoplay audio and stresses that users need control over sound, especially when audio may interfere with concentration or assistive technologies.[cite:147][cite:148][cite:151]
Sound Rules
Interface sounds off by default.
Optional toggle: Play subtle interface sounds.
Only use sounds for meaningful completions.
No page-load sound, hover sound, or constant button clicking.
Approved Sound Moments
Signup success
Upload complete
Audio ready
Optional focus session start
Phase 5: Visual Direction
The aesthetic should be digital but restrained. Research on neurodivergent-friendly design suggests avoiding visually noisy motion, excessive saturation, and other effects likely to increase sensory load.[cite:165][cite:116][cite:170]
Visual Rules
Calm dark or neutral base.
One restrained luminous accent.
Minimal motion.
Strong typography.
No rainbow explosions, smoke bursts, or pulsing default effects.
Phase 6: Trust, Privacy, And Reassurance
Users need reassurance before they upload academic or personal documents. Trust-building copy and predictable feedback reduce anxiety and help people feel safe enough to continue.[cite:174][cite:175]
Trust Copy Areas
Near signup: No spam. You can stop anytime.
Near upload: Your reading stays private and can be deleted.
Near HD option: HD is slower by design.
Near success states: You do not need to set everything up now.
Phase 7: Measurement
The product should be measured from first impression to first audio session. Onboarding and activation literature emphasizes tracking the path from signup to first meaningful value, not just page views.[cite:157][cite:158]

Success Criteria
The first release is successful when a new user can understand the promise, sign up without friction, upload a document, and reach audio playback without confusion. The experience should feel calming, obvious, and trustworthy rather than clever, flashy, or overloaded.[cite:116][cite:115][cite:174]
